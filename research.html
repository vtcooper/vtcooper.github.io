Research

I study coupled climate dynamics, including atmosphere-ocean-ice interactions, radiative feedbacks and climate sensitivity, and climate variability. I am currently investigating how climate feedbacks and Earth's energy budget have changed in the past, specifically over the Last Glacial Maximum and recent instrumental records. In this work, I am focusing on the role of spatial patterns of sea-surface temperature (SST) in determining the global radiative feedback, i.e., the "pattern effect."

Tools that I use: I run custom experiments in global climate models (using CESM), I use statistical methods (data assimilation and linear inverse models) to reconstruct and investigate past climate periods, and I analyze observations (e.g., ocean surface wave spectra from buoys and moorings) to inform modeling and improve fundamental understanding.

Polar climate has been a recurring research theme through my study of the Last Glacial Maximum (~20,000 years ago) and my work on interactions between sea ice and ocean surface waves.

SST Pattern Effect in the Paleoclimate Record

This work focuses on how the spatial pattern of sea-surface temperature (SST) impacts climate feedbacks and sensitivity ("pattern effect"). We have found that the spatial pattern of cooling during the Last Glacial Maximum (LGM) is associated with a more positive global feedback, compared to the pattern of warming we expect from a doubling of carbon dioxide (CO2). We are using atmosphere-only GCMs to quantify the LGM pattern effect. I have been running prescribed-SST experiments in CAM4 and CAM5 using SST and Sea Ice.

If we use the LGM as a constraint on the climate sensitivity that is relevant to modern-day global warming (as in Sherwood et al. 2020), we need to make an adjustment for how these different SST patterns (LGM vs. 2xCO2) drive different radiative feedbacks (and climate sensitivities). The pattern-effect adjustment applied to the LGM reduces estimates of future warming and helps constrain what could happen in the future as greenhouse gases warm the planet.

Additional planned work will explore SST pattern effects in the Pliocene (a past warm period ~3 million years ago) and the recent instrumental record (1850-present).

See my most recent poster presented at CFMIP in July 2022 [Download Poster].

Cooper, V. T., et al. (in prep). The Sea Surface Temperature Pattern Effect in the Last Glacial Maximum Reduces Climate Sensitivity Estimates.

Collaborators: Kyle Armour, Cristi Proistosescu, Greg Hakim, Yue Dong, Jessica Tierney, Matthew Osman, Natalie Burls, Daniel Amrhein, Timothy Andrews, and Yi Ming.

SST anomaly patterns from 2xCO2 compared to the Last Glacial Maximum

In atmosphere-only models with the LGM and 2xCO2 SST patterns prescribed as lower boundary conditions, we can estimate the climate feedback associated with each SST pattern. We repeat this in different atmosphere models and using different versions of LGM SST patterns, and we systematically find that the LGM feedback is less negative compared to the 2xCO2 feedback.

The pattern effect, Δλ = λ2xCO2 - λLGM, is negative. This reduces estimates of modern-day climate sensitivity based on the LGM.
